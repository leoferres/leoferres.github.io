I just got back from a 2 week trip to Argentina with the kids, and
wanted a quick, light lunch (without any meat, for obvious reasons). I
found
[this recipe](http://www.emol.com/noticias/Tendencias/2015/09/04/748237/Quinoa-Thai-6-personas.html)
and it looked simple and with only a few ingredients, so I decided to
give it a shot, though I only tried to cool Thai food once before, and
it was, well, inedible. This one is for 6 people, so it should last me
until tonight.

First off, I didn't know what "clean, rinsed and dry quinoa" meant. A
quick Google search yielded
[this page](http://www.bonappetit.com/test-kitchen/common-mistakes/article/5-most-common-mistakes-when-cooking-quinoa),
which answered my question. Below's a picture of all the ingredients:

<a href="/images/DSC_0005.jpg"><img src="/images/DSC_0005.jpg"
width="200" hspace="20" align="left"/></a>

I was mostly worried about
"[cúrcuma](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curcuma_longa)"
(Eng. [turmeric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turmeric)?, never heard
that one before), but the local supermarket carried it, so it was
ok. It's happened to me several times here in Concepción that I want
to make a dish but I could not find a key ingredient... garam masala,
or dashi are examples. (Although, in this case, they did not have any
basil left, go figure. I replaced it with dry basil and baby spinach
leaves. It was ok.)

While cooking, it was hard to know when the quinoa was ready, but it
looks like 15 is way too much wrt 1.5 cups of quinoa and 2 cups of
vegetable stock. The stock evaporated way too quickly, at least on my
stove, which has a relative high minumum temperature for the
element. So, I went with my instinct here and at the 9th minute, I
added half a cup of (hot) water. Here's what it looked like:

<a href="/images/DSC_0006.jpg"><img src="/images/DSC_0006.jpg" width="200" hspace="20" align="right"/></a>

It doesn't look like much, but at the end of it all, it tasted quite
good, so I'm pleased with that. Also, let me say that I washed the
quinoa quite well on the strainer. I tried to let dry a bit, but was a
bit too impatient. It really didn't affect the taste, as far as I can
tell, there was no soapy flavor, and no mushy texture.

There was an... incident. The coconut milk can attacked me. It wasn't
pretty. When I opened it, without knowing, the top cream had hardened,
so I pushed it *gently* towards the bottom of the can. However, the
liquid below splat all over me. I couldn't... well, reconstitute the
milk in the can, so I had to put it in a large bowl and whisk it for a
while. It ended up ok, but I was scarred for life. Below's a pic of
the reconstituted milk. Another incident involved the ginger root. For
future reference, never, and I mean never, use the smallest-factor (!)
grater to grate the root. I couldn't get it out for the life of
me. Here's what it looked like:

<a href="/images/DSC_0008.jpg"><img src="/images/DSC_0008.jpg" width="200" hspace="30" align="left"/></a>

<a href="/images/DSC_0009.jpg"><img src="/images/DSC_0009.jpg" width="200" align="left"/></a>

<a href="/images/DSC_0007.jpg"><img src="/images/curcuma.jpg" width="200" hspace="30"/></a>

That being said, I loooved the smell and taste of turmeric
(curcuma). It was delicious. Here goes to curcuma. Now that I think of
it, I should have gotten some lime. Thai food without a wedge of lime
is just not right.

**Summary**:

All in all, I ate it and it was ok. Nothing extraordinary really. It
was also a bit too sweet with the peanut butter and all. Cooking Thai
food is hard. May want to try other things for a while, and otherwise
go to the [Pad Thai](http://www.padthai.cl/) restaurant on Manuel
Montt.

**Final grade (/10)**: 5<br>
**Would eat again?**: Probably not.
