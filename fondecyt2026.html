Issues of interdisciplinarity

This project is formally submitted to the Interdisciplinary Evaluation Group as defined in Section 5.2 of the FONDECYT guidelines, reflecting the fundamental requirement for disciplinary integration to address the core research questions. The investigation of mobility-mediated health vulnerability cannot be adequately addressed through any single disciplinary lens but requires genuine synthesis of insights from epidemiology, data science, environmental science, behavioral psychology, economics, and urban planning.

The interdisciplinary approach operates through three levels of integration. Conceptual integration involves the co-development of theoretical frameworks that synthesize insights across disciplines rather than simply applying multiple disciplinary perspectives to the same problem. The project's core conceptual framework, which positions mobility as a mediating mechanism between environmental conditions and health outcomes, emerges from the intersection of epidemiological understanding of disease transmission, economic theories of spatial behavior, psychological models of risk perception and decision-making, and environmental science knowledge of exposure pathways.

Methodological integration occurs through the joint design of analytical approaches that combine methods from different disciplines in ways that create new analytical capabilities. The project's Bayesian modeling framework integrates epidemiological models of disease transmission with economic models of spatial choice, behavioral models of risk response, and environmental models of exposure dynamics. This integration is not simply a matter of running parallel analyses but involves the co-specification of model structures, prior distributions, and validation approaches that reflect insights from all contributing disciplines.

Interpretive integration ensures that results are understood and contextualized through multiple disciplinary perspectives simultaneously. The project includes formal mechanisms for interdisciplinary interpretation, including co-design workshops where team members from different disciplines jointly examine model outputs and develop explanations that synthesize insights across domains. This process is essential for ensuring that findings are not simply translated between disciplines but are understood in ways that reflect their interdisciplinary nature.

This team composition enables genuine co-construction of research approaches rather than parallel disciplinary contributions. Team members have established collaborative relationships through previous joint publications, including work on mobility patterns during COVID-19 [22], socioeconomic stratification and epidemic dynamics [23], and environmental health in Chilean cities [24]. These prior collaborations demonstrate the team's capacity for interdisciplinary integration and provide a foundation for the more ambitious integration required by this project.

The project's methodological approach reflects interdisciplinary integration at multiple levels. The Bayesian modeling framework incorporates epidemiological understanding of disease transmission processes, economic theories of spatial choice and revealed preference, psychological models of risk perception and behavioral response, and environmental science knowledge of exposure pathways and dose-response relationships. Prior distributions are developed through interdisciplinary workshops that synthesize expert knowledge across domains, while model validation incorporates criteria from multiple disciplines.

Data integration also requires interdisciplinary expertise. Mobile phone data provides insights into mobility patterns but requires careful interpretation to avoid biases and privacy violations. Environmental monitoring data must be integrated with mobility patterns to estimate individual-level exposures, requiring understanding of both environmental processes and human behavior. Health surveillance data must be interpreted in the context of healthcare access patterns and reporting biases that vary by socioeconomic position.

The project's focus on policy applications necessitates interdisciplinary integration. Effective policy recommendations must reflect understanding of epidemiological processes, environmental dynamics, behavioral responses, economic constraints, and political feasibility. The project includes formal mechanisms for stakeholder engagement that bring together researchers from different disciplines with policymakers and practitioners to ensure that analyses address real-world decision-making needs.

Training and capacity building also reflect the interdisciplinary approach. Graduate students working on the project will receive training that spans multiple disciplines, including computational methods, epidemiological theory, environmental science, and policy analysis. This training is essential for developing the next generation of researchers capable of addressing complex problems that require interdisciplinary approaches.

The project's expected outputs demonstrate interdisciplinary integration. Publications will appear in journals that span multiple disciplines, from computational epidemiology to environmental health to urban studies. The policy tools and recommendations will reflect synthesis of insights across disciplines rather than disciplinary-specific advice. The international workshop will bring together researchers from different disciplines to advance understanding of mobility-health dynamics as an interdisciplinary research domain.

This interdisciplinary approach is not simply a methodological choice but a fundamental requirement for addressing the project's core research questions. Understanding how mobility mediates the relationship between environmental conditions and health outcomes requires integration of insights about human behavior, environmental processes, disease transmission, and social structures that cannot be achieved through any single disciplinary perspective. The project's success depends on genuine interdisciplinary collaboration that creates new knowledge through the synthesis of disciplinary insights rather than their simple aggregation.

Integrated Conceptual Framework

The theoretical foundation of this project rests on understanding mobility as a structured social process that mediates the relationship between environmental conditions and health outcomes. Traditional environmental health research treats exposure as a function of residential location, workplace characteristics, or simple proximity to pollution sources. This approach, while valuable, fails to capture the dynamic nature of exposure patterns and the ways in which mobility routines create complex, temporally varying exposure profiles [14]. Our conceptual framework builds on three key theoretical insights. First, mobility patterns are not random but reflect underlying social and economic structures that shape access to transportation options, employment locations, and residential choices [15]. These structures create what we term "mobility capital", the ability to choose when, where, and how to move through urban space in ways that minimize exposure to environmental hazards while maintaining access to economic and social opportunities [16]. Second, environmental exposures occur not just at fixed locations but along movement pathways, creating exposure networks that connect seemingly distant locations through shared transportation infrastructure [17]. Third, health outcomes emerge from the interaction between exposure patterns and individual vulnerability, which itself varies by socioeconomic position, age, pre-existing health conditions, and access to healthcare [18]. The framework integrates these insights through three mechanisms. {\bf Differential exposure networks} emerge when socioeconomic stratification creates distinct mobility patterns that result in different environmental exposure profiles. For example, individuals who rely on public transportation may experience higher pollution exposure during commuting but lower exposure at work locations, while those who drive private vehicles may have lower commuting exposure but higher exposure if they work in industrial areas. These differences create parallel exposure networks within the same urban area, each with distinct risk profiles. {\bf Temporal amplification effects} occur when synchronized daily routines create periods of concentrated vulnerability. School schedules, work shifts, and transportation timetables create temporal clustering of exposure that can amplify health impacts beyond what would be expected. For instance, if pollution peaks coincide with morning commuting periods, the health impact may be greater than if the same pollution levels occurred when fewer people were in transit. This temporal dimension of exposure has received limited attention in environmental health research but may be critical for understanding acute health impacts. {\bf Adaptive reorganization processes} describe how mobility systems respond to infrastructure changes, policy interventions, or external shocks. While individual mobility patterns may appear highly variable, aggregate patterns often exhibit strong regularities that persist even through major disruptions [19]. Understanding these adaptive processes is essential for predicting how infrastructure investments or policy changes will alter health outcomes. The framework explicitly incorporates the role of structural inequalities in shaping these mechanisms. Socioeconomic position influences not only where people live and work but also their transportation options, schedule flexibility, and ability to avoid high-exposure periods or locations. These inequalities are not simply individual characteristics but reflect broader patterns of urban development, transportation investment, and labor market structure that create systematic differences in mobility capital across population groups. Environmental conditions interact with these mobility patterns through multiple pathways. Air pollution exposure depends not only on ambient concentrations but also on time spent in different microenvironments (such as the metro tunnels), physical activity levels during exposure, and access to protective measures such as air conditioning or high-quality public transportation [20]. Climate variability affects mobility patterns both directly, through impacts on transportation systems, and indirectly, through changes in activity patterns and time spent outdoors [21]. Understanding these interactions requires moving beyond simple exposure metrics to consider the full pathway from environmental conditions through mobility patterns to health outcomes. The framework also recognizes the dynamic nature of these relationships. Mobility patterns evolve in response to changing environmental conditions, infrastructure development, and social and economic changes. These processes create feedback loops where health outcomes influence mobility choices, which in turn affect exposure patterns and subsequent health risks. For example, individuals with respiratory conditions may alter their commuting routes or timing to avoid high-pollution periods, creating selection effects that complicate simple causal inference. This conceptual foundation guides the development of specific hypotheses and analytical approaches. Rather than treating mobility, environmental exposure, and health outcomes as separate domains, the framework emphasizes their integration through structured social processes. This perspective enables analysis of how interventions in one domain—such as transportation infrastructure development or air quality regulations—may have cascading effects across the entire system. The Chilean context provides particularly rich opportunities for testing this framework. The country's marked socioeconomic stratification creates clear differences in mobility capital across population groups. Santiago's geography, with pollution trapped in a valley surrounded by mountains, creates sharp spatial gradients in environmental conditions that interact with mobility patterns in complex ways. The city's ongoing metro expansion provides natural experiments for studying how infrastructure changes alter mobility-health dynamics. Additionally, Chile's comprehensive health surveillance system and growing availability of environmental monitoring data enable empirical testing of the framework's key predictions.
